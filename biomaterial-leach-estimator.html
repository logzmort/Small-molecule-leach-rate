Model, equations & literature benchmarking

1 — Free-water diffusivity (Stokes–Einstein + temperature). Each molecule carries a measured aqueous diffusivity D₀ at 25 °C. Temperature is applied through Stokes–Einstein, D₀(T)=D₀(298)·(T/298)·(η₂₉₈/η_T), with water viscosity from a Vogel correlation η(T)=2.414e-5·10^(247.8/(T−140)) Pa·s (T in K).

2 — Obstruction in the network. The reduction of diffusivity inside the swollen matrix combines four physically-motivated factors:

D_eff = D₀(T) · f_MM(φ) · f_cryst(χ) · f_xl · f_matrix
  • Mackie–Meares polymer obstruction: f_MM = ((1−φ)/(1+φ))² — the classic result for a small solute navigating around impenetrable polymer segments of volume fraction φ.
  • Maxwell / crystalline obstruction for impermeable β-sheet or crystalline domains: f_cryst = (1−χ)/(1+χ/2).
  • Crosslink tightening of mesh: f_xl = exp(−2·xl).
  • Matrix factor for residual tortuosity/hydrophobic partitioning (chitosan ≈ 1.0, silk ≈ 0.6).

3 — Ionic strength (polyelectrolyte deswelling). Chitosan is a polycation; added salt screens the fixed charges and collapses the network, raising the effective φ and slowing release. Modeled phenomenologically as φ_eff = φ·(1 + s·log₁₀(I/150)) with s ≈ 0.35 (chitosan) or ≈ 0.05 (near-neutral silk), clamped to a physical range. Lower salt → more swelling → faster leaching.

4 — Release kinetics (Crank exact solutions, perfect-sink desorption). Each geometry has its own series; the characteristic length that scales the dimensionless time τ = D_eff·t/ℓ² depends on the shape:

slab (thickness L):  Mₜ/M∞ = 1 − Σₙ [8/((2n+1)²π²)]·exp(−(2n+1)²π²·τ),  ℓ=L   sphere (radius a):  Mₜ/M∞ = 1 − (6/π²)·Σₙ (1/n²)·exp(−n²π²·τ),  ℓ=a   cylinder (radius a): Mₜ/M∞ = 1 − Σₙ (4/βₙ²)·exp(−βₙ²·τ),  J₀(βₙ)=0,  ℓ=a   cube (edge a):  Mₜ/M∞ = 1 − [R_slab(τ)]³  (product of three 1-D sheets),  ℓ=a

Half-times scale as t₅₀ ≈ k·ℓ²/D_eff with k ≈ 0.049 (slab), 0.063 (cylinder), 0.030 (sphere) — for the same size, a sphere empties fastest because it has the most surface per volume. The plotted leach flux is d(Mₜ)/dt normalized by the true release surface S, using your loading: areal load M∞/S = C₀·(V/S), where V/S = L/2 (slab), a/2 (cylinder), a/3 (sphere), a/6 (cube).

Benchmarking. Reported effective diffusivities for small polar solutes (glycerol, urea, sugars, small drugs) in swollen chitosan and silk-fibroin films fall in roughly 10⁻¹³ – 10⁻¹¹ m²/s, i.e. 1–3 orders of magnitude below their free-water values (~10⁻⁹ m²/s), consistent with obstruction-scaling theory. The panel flags whether your inputs land in, above, or below that experimentally-observed band. Representative sources: Crank, The Mathematics of Diffusion (1975); Mackie & Meares, Proc. R. Soc. A (1955); Amsden, Macromolecules (1998) obstruction review; glycerol-plasticized chitosan film studies; silk-fibroin controlled-release literature.

Assumptions/limits: homogeneous non-swelling (pseudo-steady) slab, dilute non-interacting solute, perfect sink, no burst/erosion/convective washout, temperature-independent φ. Use for design intuition and relative comparison, not regulatory prediction.
